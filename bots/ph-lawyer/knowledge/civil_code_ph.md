# Civil Code of the Philippines — Key Provisions
# Republic Act No. 386 (as amended)
# Knowledge Base for AbugadoAI

---

## PART I: GENERAL PROVISIONS

### Sources of Philippine Law
- The Civil Code governs personal and family relations, property, contracts, and obligations
- Enacted in 1950, it draws from Spanish Civil Code and American common law
- Supplemented by special laws (e.g., Family Code of 1987, which superseded many civil code family provisions)

### Persons and Family (Book I)
**Natural Persons:**
- Civil personality begins at birth (Art. 40); an unborn child has rights if born alive
- Age of majority: 18 years (RA 6809)
- Full civil capacity at age 18

**Juridical Persons (Corporations/Organizations):**
- Have civil personality separate from members
- Governed by Corporation Code (Revised Corporation Code, RA 11232)

## PART II: FAMILY LAW (Family Code of the Philippines, EO 209)

### Marriage
**Requirements for a Valid Marriage:**
- Both parties must be male and female (same-sex marriage is not recognized)
- Legal capacity: at least 18 years old (parental consent required for 18–21)
- Free consent of both parties
- Marriage must be performed before a solemnizing officer (judge, priest, mayor, etc.)
- Valid marriage license (except for certain exemptions: marriages of exceptional character)

**Void Marriages (no legal effect from the start):**
- Contracted by a person below 18 years old
- Bigamous or polygamous marriages
- Contracted through mistake of identity
- Psychological incapacity under Article 36 of the Family Code
- Incestuous marriages (between ascendants/descendants, siblings)
- Public policy marriages (between step-parents/stepchildren, etc.)

**Voidable Marriages (valid until annulled):**
- Lack of parental consent (if 18–21 at time of marriage)
- Either party was of unsound mind
- Fraud (concealment of criminal conviction, pregnancy by another man, STD, drug addiction)
- Force, intimidation, or undue influence
- Physical incapacity to consummate the marriage (impotence)
- STD found to be serious and incurable

**Annulment Process:**
- Filed in the Regional Trial Court (Family Court) of the province/city where either party resides
- Petition must include grounds, proof, and affidavits
- OSG (Office of the Solicitor General) and public prosecutor participate to prevent collusion
- Children: legitimacy is NOT affected by annulment; parental authority must be addressed
- Property: dissolved as if marriage ended (liquidation of community or conjugal property)

### Legal Separation
- Does NOT dissolve the marriage bond — parties cannot remarry
- Grounds: repeated physical violence, drug addiction, infidelity, attempt on life, lesbianism/homosexuality, drug use, etc.
- Must file within 5 years of the occurrence of the ground
- Court may order the innocent spouse to receive larger share of net profits

### Psychological Incapacity (Art. 36, Family Code)
- Most common ground for annulment in the Philippines
- SC Ruling in Tan-Andal v. Andal (2021): psychological incapacity need not be a mental illness; it is a personal condition that renders a party incapable of fulfilling marital obligations
- Must be proven by clear and convincing evidence
- Need not be present at the time of marriage; may be manifested after

## PART III: PROPERTY LAW

### Classification of Property (Art. 414)
**Immovable Property (Real Property):**
- Land, buildings, constructions permanently attached to land
- Trees, plants, growing fruits
- Mines, quarries, mineral resources

**Movable Property (Personal Property):**
- Everything not classified as immovable
- Includes shares of stock, obligations/bonds, intellectual property

### Ownership
- **Full ownership** includes right to enjoy, dispose, and recover the property
- Limitations: cannot abuse rights, must not harm others or the state
- **Co-ownership**: when two or more persons own the same undivided thing
  - Each co-owner may use the property proportionally
  - Share is presumed equal unless otherwise proven
  - Co-owner can demand partition at any time (Art. 494)

### Property Regimes in Marriage
**Absolute Community of Property (ACP)** — default regime after August 3, 1988:
- All property owned by spouses before and during marriage is community property
- Exceptions: property received by gift or inheritance, personal belongings
- Upon dissolution, debts are paid first, then net remainder divided equally

**Conjugal Partnership of Gains (CPG)** — applies to marriages before August 3, 1988 (or by agreement):
- Each spouse retains ownership of property brought into marriage
- Only fruits/income of property and property acquired during marriage are conjugal
- Upon dissolution: debts paid, then net gains divided equally

**Complete Separation of Property** — must be stipulated in a prenuptial agreement

### Land Ownership
- Only Filipino citizens and corporations (≥60% Filipino-owned) may own land
- Foreign nationals may NOT own land (but may own condominiums up to 40% of units)
- Foreigners may lease land for up to 50 years, renewable for 25 years (RA 7652)

### Lease of Real Property
- Lease contract may be oral or written
- If lease is for more than 1 year, it must be in writing to be enforceable
- Lessee's rights: quiet enjoyment, use the property for agreed purpose, make necessary repairs
- **Rent Control Act (RA 9653):** applies to residential units with rent not exceeding ₱10,000/month (NCR) or ₱5,000/month (other areas)
  - Annual rent increase capped at 7%
  - Lessor cannot increase rent more than once a year
  - Ejectment requires proper notice and valid ground

## PART IV: OBLIGATIONS AND CONTRACTS

### Obligations
**Essential Elements:**
1. Active subject (creditor — person entitled to demand)
2. Passive subject (debtor — person bound to fulfill)
3. Object (the prestation — to give, to do, or not to do)
4. Juridical tie (source of obligation)

**Sources of Obligations (Art. 1157):**
1. Law
2. Contracts
3. Quasi-contracts (e.g., solutio indebiti — mistaken payment)
4. Acts or omissions punished by law (delicts)
5. Quasi-delicts (negligence causing damage)

**Extinguishment of Obligations:**
- Payment or performance
- Loss of the thing due (fortuitous event)
- Condonation or remission (creditor forgives the debt)
- Confusion or merger (creditor and debtor become the same person)
- Compensation (both parties are creditors and debtors of each other)
- Novation (replacing an old obligation with a new one)

### Contracts
**Essential Requisites (Art. 1318):**
1. **Consent** — meeting of minds, free from fraud/intimidation/mistake
2. **Object** — lawful subject matter (cannot be contrary to law, morals, public order)
3. **Cause/Consideration** — the why of the contract (must be lawful)

**Classification:**
- **Nominate contracts**: defined and regulated by law (sales, lease, loan, partnership, etc.)
- **Innominate contracts**: not specifically regulated ("I give that you give," etc.)

**Defective Contracts:**
- **Void/Inexistent**: no legal effect from the start (e.g., lacks essential requisite, object is illegal)
- **Voidable**: valid until annulled (consent vitiated by mistake, fraud, violence, intimidation)
- **Unenforceable**: cannot be enforced unless ratified (violates Statute of Frauds)
- **Rescissible**: valid but can be rescinded due to damage to one of the parties or third persons

**Statute of Frauds (Art. 1403):**
The following contracts must be in writing to be enforceable:
- Agreements not to be performed within one year
- Special promise to answer for debt/default of another (surety)
- Contract made in consideration of marriage (except mutual promise to marry)
- Lease of real property for more than one year
- Sale of real property or an interest therein
- Sale of goods worth ₱500 or more

### Sale of Property
- Contract of sale: seller transfers ownership; buyer pays the price
- Ownership transfers upon delivery (not upon signing the contract)
- Warranty against eviction: seller must defend buyer if a third party claims the property
- Warranty against hidden defects: seller is liable for defects not apparent to buyer

## PART V: SUCCESSION (Inheritance)

### Testate vs. Intestate Succession
**Testate Succession** — with a valid will:
- Must be executed in writing, signed by testator, and attested by 3 witnesses
- **Notarial will**: signed on each page, attested, notarized
- **Holographic will**: entirely written, dated, and signed by hand by testator; no witness required

**Intestate Succession** — without a will or will is invalid:
- Estate distributed according to the Civil Code's order of succession

**Order of Intestate Heirs:**
1. Legitimate children (and descendants)
2. Legitimate parents and ascendants (if no children)
3. Illegitimate children
4. Surviving spouse
5. Collateral relatives (brothers, sisters, nephews, nieces)
6. The State (if no other heirs)

### Legitime (Compulsory Heirs)
Compulsory heirs cannot be deprived of their minimum share (legitime):

| Heir | Legitime |
|------|----------|
| Legitimate children | 1/2 of estate (divided equally among all) |
| Surviving spouse (with children) | Equal share to each child's legitime |
| Illegitimate children | 1/2 of legitimate child's share each |
| Parents (if no children) | 1/2 of estate |

**Free portion**: The testator can freely dispose of the estate after satisfying the legitime.

### Estate Tax
- Estate tax rate: **6%** of net estate (RA 10963 — TRAIN Law)
- Filing deadline: 1 year from date of death
- Estate tax amnesty was available under RA 11213 (extended); check current BIR advisories

## PART VI: TORTS AND DAMAGES

### Quasi-Delicts (Art. 2176)
- A person who causes damage to another through fault or negligence is liable
- No criminal intent required (unlike delicts)
- Must prove: (a) damage suffered, (b) fault/negligence, (c) causal connection

### Types of Damages
- **Actual/Compensatory**: proven financial loss (medical bills, lost income, property damage)
- **Moral**: mental anguish, serious anxiety, wounded feelings — courts have wide discretion
- **Nominal**: to vindicate a right violated, even without proof of actual loss
- **Temperate/Moderate**: when actual damage cannot be precisely determined
- **Exemplary/Punitive**: to set an example, awarded in addition to other damages
- **Attorney's Fees**: may be awarded in cases where it is just and equitable

### Prescription (Statute of Limitations)
| Type of Action | Prescriptive Period |
|----------------|---------------------|
| Written contracts | 10 years |
| Oral contracts | 6 years |
| Quasi-contracts | 6 years |
| Quasi-delicts (torts) | 4 years |
| Actions for recovery of real property | 30 years (ordinary); 10 years (extraordinary possession) |
| Actions on judgment | 10 years |
| Criminal cases vary by offense | — |
