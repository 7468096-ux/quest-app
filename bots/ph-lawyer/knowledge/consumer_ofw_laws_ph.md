## PART I: CONSUMER ACT OF THE PHILIPPINES (RA 7394)

### Overview
The Consumer Act of 1992 protects consumers against hazardous products, deceptive practices, and substandard goods and services. Enforced by the **Department of Trade and Industry (DTI)**.

### Consumer Rights (Art. 4)
1. **Right to basic needs** — access to essential goods and services
2. **Right to safety** — protection against hazardous products
3. **Right to information** — truthful, honest labeling and advertising
4. **Right to choose** — access to competitive products
5. **Right to representation** — participation in government policy
6. **Right to redress** — fair settlement of just claims
7. **Right to consumer education** — knowledge to make informed choices
8. **Right to a healthy environment** — protection from environmental hazards

### Product Quality and Safety
**Mandatory standards:**
- Products must meet the Philippine National Standards (PNS) set by the Bureau of Product Standards (BPS)
- Importation of substandard products is prohibited
- Manufacturers must certify safety compliance for certain products (electronics, toys, appliances)

**Recall and warning:**
- DTI can order a product recall if found to be hazardous
- Consumers who suffer harm from defective products may claim damages

### Product Warranty
**Implied Warranty of Merchantability:**
- All goods sold must be fit for their ordinary purpose
- Applies even if not expressly stated

**Express Warranty:**
- Any written or oral guarantee by the seller or manufacturer
- Must be honored as stated

**Duration of Implied Warranty:**
- Minimum implied warranty: **30 days for used goods**; no fixed period for new goods (but must be fit for purpose)
- Most electronics come with a minimum **1-year manufacturer's warranty**

### Labeling Requirements
Products must have labels in English or Filipino showing:
- Product name, brand
- Manufacturer name and address
- Net quantity (in metric units)
- Lot/batch number
- Expiry date (for food, medicine, cosmetics)
- Ingredients (for food and cosmetics)
- Warnings, hazard symbols (for chemicals)

### Advertising and Unfair Trade Practices
**Prohibited acts:**
- False or misleading advertising
- Price manipulation or deceptive pricing
- Bait-and-switch selling
- Pyramid selling schemes
- Selling below cost to eliminate competition (predatory pricing)

**Online selling rules (E-Commerce Act, RA 8792):**
- Online sellers must honor prices displayed
- Return/exchange policy must be disclosed
- No deceptive product descriptions

### Filing a Consumer Complaint
**Steps:**
1. Try to resolve directly with the seller/service provider (send written complaint)
2. If unresolved, file with **DTI Fair Trade Enforcement Bureau**:
   - DTI Hotline: **1-DTI (1-384)**
   - Email: fteb@dti.gov.ph
   - Online: DTI Complaints and Action Center (CAC)
3. For fraud/scams, file with the **NBI (National Bureau of Investigation)** or PNP Anti-Cybercrime Group

**Small Claims Court:**
- For money claims of ₱400,000 and below (revised amount under AM 08-8-7-SC)
- No lawyer required, quick resolution, filing fee minimal

## PART II: RENT CONTROL ACT (RA 9653)

### Coverage
Applies to **residential units** with monthly rent of:
- ₱10,000 and below — **Metro Manila and highly urbanized cities**
- ₱5,000 and below — **other areas**

### Key Protections for Tenants
- **Annual rent increase cap: 7%** (lessor can only increase once per year)
- **No arbitrary ejectment** — only for valid grounds
- **Security deposit:** Advance payment + security deposit cannot exceed 2 months advance + 2 months deposit (total 4 months)

### Valid Grounds for Ejectment (Lessor's Right)
1. Non-payment of rent for **3 consecutive months**
2. Lessor needs property for **own use or family's use**
3. Lessor needs to **demolish or repair** the property (with HLURB/permit)
4. **Expiry of lease contract** (lessee does not renew)
5. Lessee is using property for **illegal purposes**
6. **Subleasing** without lessor's written consent

### Ejectment Procedure
- Written notice to vacate (at least **3 months** for grounds 2 and 3)
- If tenant refuses: file **unlawful detainer (ejectment)** case at Municipal Trial Court
- **Forced eviction without court order is illegal** — violation of tenant's rights
- Cutting utilities (water, electricity) to force eviction is prohibited

### Tenant's Obligations
- Pay rent on time
- Not sublease without consent
- Maintain property in good condition
- Not use property for unauthorized purposes

## PART III: ANTI-VIOLENCE AGAINST WOMEN AND CHILDREN (RA 9262)

### Coverage
Protects women and their children from physical, sexual, psychological, and economic abuse by a spouse, former spouse, or partner.

### Forms of Violence Covered
- **Physical:** Bodily harm, assault, battery
- **Sexual:** Any sexual act without consent, sexual harassment
- **Psychological:** Intimidation, harassment, stalking, public humiliation, controlling behavior
- **Economic:** Deprivation of financial support, destruction of property, preventing victim from earning income

### Remedies Available
**Barangay Protection Order (BPO):**
- Issued by the Barangay Captain within 24 hours
- Valid for 15 days
- Orders abuser to stop the violence and vacate shared residence

**Temporary Protection Order (TPO):**
- Issued by the court within 24 hours of application
- Valid for 30 days

**Permanent Protection Order (PPO):**
- Issued after full hearing
- Permanently restrains abuser

### VAWC Leave
- Female employees who are VAWC victims are entitled to **10 days paid leave** per year (no need for extended absence to qualify)
- Cannot be charged against annual leave

### Filing a VAWC Complaint
1. Go to the **Barangay Hall** (for BPO) or **Women and Children Protection Desk at PNP**
2. File at the **Regional Trial Court (RTC)** for TPO/PPO
3. The **DSWD (Dept. of Social Welfare and Development)** provides shelter and support

## PART IV: OVERSEAS FILIPINO WORKERS (OFW) RIGHTS

### Governing Law: Migrant Workers and Overseas Filipinos Act (RA 10022)

### Pre-Deployment
- All overseas employment must be through **POEA-accredited recruitment agencies** (now DMW)
- Recruitment fees: Cannot exceed one month salary
- Contract must be verified by POEA before departure
- All OFWs must have **OWWA (Overseas Workers Welfare Administration)** membership

### Standard Employment Contract
Minimum protections regardless of host country:
- Guaranteed salary (no deductions unless authorized)
- Free food or food allowance
- Free accommodation (or allowance)
- Free medical/dental care
- Repatriation upon contract expiry at employer's expense
- Travel insurance

### Illegal Recruitment (RA 10022, Art. XIII)
- Deploying workers without POEA/DMW license
- Charging excessive fees
- Misrepresentation of job offers
- **Penalties:** 6 years + 1 day to life imprisonment; fines up to ₱2 million
- **Large-scale illegal recruitment** (3 or more victims): non-bailable offense

### Rights Upon Return (Repatriation)
- Government shoulder cost of repatriation if employer refuses
- OWWA provides reintegration assistance (livelihood, training, loans)
- Distressed OFWs: contact **DFA (Department of Foreign Affairs)** hotline: +632-8834-4000 or the nearest Philippine Embassy/Consulate

### POEA/DMW Complaints
- File at DMW main office or regional offices
- Hotline: **1348**
- For abuse abroad: contact the **Philippine Overseas Labor Office (POLO)** at the embassy

### OFW Families
- OFW children have priority in state scholarship programs
- OFW spouses may file for separate property administration if OFW is incapacitated

## PART V: LANDLORD-TENANT DISPUTES AND BARANGAY JUSTICE

### Katarungang Pambarangay (RA 7160 — Local Government Code)

**Mandatory Conciliation:**
Before filing cases in court, disputes between residents of the same municipality involving:
- Disputes between individuals (not corporations)
- Where parties reside in the same barangay or adjacent barangays

**The Lupon (Barangay Justice Committee):**
- Composed of 10-20 members chosen by the Barangay Captain
- **Pangkat ng Tagapagkasundo** (3-member panel) mediates

**Process:**
1. File complaint with the Barangay Hall (Lupon Secretary)
2. Mediation by the Barangay Captain (15-day period)
3. If unresolved, referred to the Pangkat (15+15 days)
4. If settled: **Amicable Settlement** — has the force and effect of a final judgment after 10 days
5. If not settled: **Certificate to File Action** issued → parties can now go to court

**Exemptions from Barangay Conciliation:**
- One party is not a resident of the Philippines
- Disputes involving real property (ejectment) with parties from different cities/municipalities
- Criminal cases where one party may be arrested
- Habeas corpus, emergencies, provisional remedies
