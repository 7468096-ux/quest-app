## GRAMMAR REVIEW

### Parts of Speech

**Nouns** — names a person, place, thing, idea
- Proper nouns (capitalized): Manila, Rizal, Pasig River
- Common nouns: city, hero, river
- Collective nouns: class, flock, committee (take singular or plural verb depending on context)
- Abstract nouns: love, freedom, democracy

**Pronouns — Subject/Object/Possessive**
| Subject | Object | Possessive Adj | Possessive Pronoun |
|---------|--------|----------------|---------------------|
| I | me | my | mine |
| you | you | your | yours |
| he | him | his | his |
| she | her | her | hers |
| we | us | our | ours |
| they | them | their | theirs |

**Verb Tenses — Key Rules**
- Simple Present: habitual actions, general truths → "She studies every day."
- Simple Past: completed action at a specific time → "She studied yesterday."
- Present Perfect: action with present relevance → "She has studied for 3 hours."
- Past Perfect: action before another past action → "She had studied before the exam."
- Future: plans/predictions → "She will study tomorrow."

**Subject-Verb Agreement Rules**
1. Compound subjects with "and" → plural verb: "Juan and Maria ARE here."
2. Compound subjects with "or/nor" → verb agrees with the nearer subject: "Neither the students nor the teacher IS absent."
3. Collective nouns (class, team, family) → usually singular in American English: "The class IS doing well."
4. Each, every, either, neither → singular: "Each student HAS a book."
5. There is/are → verb agrees with the subject that follows: "There ARE many ways."
6. Intervening phrases don't affect agreement: "The box of chocolates IS on the table."

### Common Grammar Errors (UPCAT Favorites)

**1. Dangling Modifiers**
❌ "Walking to school, the rain started."
✅ "Walking to school, I was caught in the rain."
Rule: The participial phrase must logically modify the subject.

**2. Pronoun-Antecedent Agreement**
❌ "Everyone must bring their own pencil." (technically incorrect but widely accepted)
✅ "Everyone must bring his or her own pencil."
Modern usage: "their" as singular is increasingly accepted.

**3. Parallel Structure**
❌ "She likes reading, to write, and swimming."
✅ "She likes reading, writing, and swimming."
Rule: Items in a series must be in the same grammatical form.

**4. Misplaced Modifiers**
❌ "She almost drove her children to school every day." (meaning: she nearly drove, but didn't)
✅ "She drove her children to school almost every day." (most days)

**5. Comparative vs. Superlative**
- 2 items → comparative: better, faster, more beautiful
- 3+ items → superlative: best, fastest, most beautiful
❌ "Between the two options, this is the best."
✅ "Between the two options, this is the better."

### Analogies — UPCAT/ACET Strategy
Format: WORD A : WORD B :: WORD C : WORD D
Types:
1. **Part-to-whole**: Petal : Flower :: Chapter : Book
2. **Cause-effect**: Fire : Smoke :: Exercise : Sweat
3. **Synonym**: Happy : Joyful :: Sad : Melancholy
4. **Antonym**: Hot : Cold :: Brave : Cowardly
5. **Object-function**: Pen : Write :: Knife : Cut
6. **Worker-tool**: Carpenter : Hammer :: Surgeon : Scalpel
7. **Category-member**: Fish : Salmon :: Bird : Eagle
8. **Degree**: Warm : Hot :: Dislike : Hate

## READING COMPREHENSION

### The 5 Question Types
1. **Main Idea** — "What is the passage mainly about?"
   → Look at first and last paragraphs; ignore specific details
2. **Detail/Fact** — "According to the passage..."
   → Scan and locate; don't rely on memory
3. **Inference** — "It can be inferred that..."
   → What's implied but not directly stated; use logic
4. **Vocabulary in Context** — "The word X in paragraph 2 means..."
   → Use context clues; consider the entire sentence
5. **Author's Purpose/Tone** — "The author's attitude toward... is..."
   → Look for loaded words, positive/negative connotations

### Reading Strategy: RACE
- **R**ead the questions FIRST (before the passage)
- **A**ctively annotate as you read (underline main ideas)
- **C**heck back to the passage for each answer
- **E**liminate wrong choices (process of elimination)

## CRITICAL THINKING

### Types of Logical Fallacies (ACET loves these)
1. **Ad Hominem** — attacking the person instead of the argument
2. **Straw Man** — misrepresenting an argument to knock it down
3. **False Dilemma** — only presenting two options when more exist
4. **Slippery Slope** — assuming one event leads to extreme consequences
5. **Bandwagon** — "everyone is doing it, so it must be right"
6. **Circular Reasoning** — the conclusion is used as a premise
7. **Appeal to Authority** — citing authority as evidence without proof

### Argument Analysis
For "Which best supports/weakens the argument?" questions:
- Identify the CLAIM (main conclusion)
- Identify the EVIDENCE (supporting facts)
- Find the ASSUMPTION (unstated link between evidence and claim)
- A statement WEAKENS if it contradicts the assumption or evidence
- A statement STRENGTHENS if it supports the assumption or provides additional evidence

## SENTENCE COMPLETION STRATEGIES
1. Look for SIGNAL words:
   - Contrast signals: however, but, although, despite, yet → opposite meaning needed
   - Continuation signals: furthermore, also, similarly, likewise → same direction meaning
   - Cause-effect signals: therefore, thus, because, since, consequently
2. Use the overall TONE of the sentence
3. Try each answer choice in context

## PRACTICE QUESTIONS

**Grammar:**
Q: Choose the correct sentence:
a) Neither the mayor nor the councilors was present.
b) Neither the mayor nor the councilors were present.
c) Neither the mayor nor the councilors has been present.
Answer: **b)** — verb agrees with the nearer subject "councilors" (plural) = "were"

**Analogy:**
Q: SURGEON : SCALPEL :: PAINTER : ___
a) canvas  b) brush  c) gallery  d) artwork
Answer: **b) brush** — worker-to-tool relationship

**Reading:**
Q: If a passage describes rising sea levels, melting ice caps, and increased storm frequency, then asks "What can be inferred?", the answer likely relates to:
→ Climate change/global warming effects (inference from evidence pattern)

## UPCAT ENGLISH TEST TIPS
- **Language Proficiency subtest**: 60-70 items, ~45 minutes → focus on grammar and vocabulary
- **Reading Comprehension subtest**: 60 items, ~60 minutes → read efficiently, skim passages
- Common trap: answers that are "true" but don't answer the question asked
- In inference questions: avoid answers that are too extreme ("always," "never," "all")
- For analogies: state the relationship clearly before looking at choices: "A scalpel is the tool a surgeon uses"
